What to Look for in Senior Dog Food

1. High-Quality Protein

Senior dogs need high-quality, easily digestible protein to maintain muscle mass — even more than you might think. Look for a named protein source (chicken, beef, salmon) as the first ingredient. Avoid foods where "meat by-products" or corn appear first.

2. Reduced Calories

Senior dogs are typically less active and have slower metabolisms. A food with slightly fewer calories helps prevent obesity, which is the #1 health problem in senior dogs and worsens joint issues, heart disease, and diabetes.

3. Joint Support (Glucosamine & Chondroitin)

Most senior dogs experience some degree of joint stiffness or arthritis. Foods fortified with glucosamine and chondroitin — or EPA/DHA from fish oil — help maintain cartilage and reduce inflammation.

4. Digestive Health

Aging dogs often have more sensitive digestive systems. Look for foods with quality fiber sources (beet pulp, pumpkin) and probiotics to support gut health and regular bowel movements.

⚠️ Warning

If your senior dog suddenly loses appetite, loses weight rapidly, or shows digestive issues, consult your veterinarian before switching foods. These can be symptoms of underlying conditions that require medical attention.

Frequently Asked Questions

When should I switch my dog to senior food?
Generally, large breeds (over 50 lbs) should transition to senior food around age 6-7. Small breeds (under 20 lbs) are considered senior around age 10-11. Your vet can advise based on your specific dog's health and weight.
Is grain-free food better for senior dogs?
Not necessarily. The FDA investigated a potential link between grain-free diets and dilated cardiomyopathy (DCM) in dogs. Most veterinary cardiologists currently recommend sticking to traditional grain-inclusive foods unless your dog has a diagnosed grain allergy.
How much should I feed my senior dog?
Follow the feeding guidelines on the bag based on your dog's current weight, then adjust based on body condition. A healthy senior dog should have a visible waist and ribs you can feel but not see. When in doubt, ask your vet.
Should senior dogs eat wet or dry food?
Both are fine. Wet food can be beneficial for dogs with dental pain or those who need extra hydration. Dry food is easier to store and generally better for dental health. Many owners mix both, which is also a great option.
